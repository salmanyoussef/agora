\begin{abstract}
Open government data portals excel at publishing catalogues, not at answering questions: users still bridge metadata search, file download, and analysis by hand. \textbf{AGORA} (Agent-based Government Open-data Reasoning Assistant) is a \emph{framework} for closing that gap with a single, transparent pipeline from natural language to grounded answers over portal data.
At a high level, the system decomposes a question for catalogue discovery, retrieves candidate datasets with hybrid semantic--lexical search, filters and routes each dataset to descriptive (passage-style) or analytic (structured, tool-supported) evidence, and synthesizes one response with explicit sources.
We instantiate the framework on France's data.gouv.fr ($\sim$73k datasets) to show that treating \emph{catalogue search} and \emph{file-level reasoning} as one coordinated IR--RAG problem yields a reusable blueprint for accessible open-data access, follow-on research, and adaptations to other jurisdictions---with implementation detail deferred to our open codebase.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

National open-data portals list tens of thousands of datasets in many formats, yet they remain \emph{discovery} systems: turning a question into retrieval over the catalogue, fetching the right files, and producing a grounded answer is still the user's burden. In IR terms the catalogue is a large, noisy index; in practice, non-experts lack a single path from intent to evidence.

We present \textbf{AGORA} (Agent-based Government Open-data Reasoning Assistant) as a \textbf{framework} for that end-to-end path~\cite{li2024multiagent, yu2025agent}. The core idea is to treat portal APIs and an embedded catalogue as \emph{first-level retrieval}, then selected datasets as \emph{second-level evidence}---either text-oriented (summaries, passages) or structure-oriented (tables, records, light computation)---and to wrap both in a linear, auditable agentic workflow with explicit attribution. The same pattern is portable to other API-backed portals: only ingestion and prompts are jurisdiction-specific; the stages (plan, search, select, execute, synthesize) stay stable.

Our reference study uses France's data.gouv.fr ($\sim$73k datasets). In short, we contribute:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A \textbf{unified problem framing}: open-data QA as \emph{two-level} retrieval (metadata $\rightarrow$ resources) plus grounded generation, with hybrid catalogue search and a selector that both filters noise and routes each dataset to the appropriate evidence mode.
  \item A \textbf{deliberate split between ``overview'' and ``analytics'' evidence}: passage-style grounding when a sample of text suffices, and tool- or code-assisted reasoning when the task requires search or aggregation over structured content---without ad hoc tool loops beyond bounded execution.
  \item A \textbf{reusable architectural blueprint}---figures, challenge mapping, and open implementation---for researchers and practitioners who want systemic, accessible access to open government data rather than a single-portal demo.
\end{itemize}

Section~\ref{sec:related} situates the work. Section~\ref{sec:protocol} formalizes the AGORA protocol (workflow, state, aggregation). Section~\ref{sec:architecture} presents figures and interfaces. Sections~\ref{sec:challenges}--\ref{sec:synthesis} walk the stages; Section~\ref{sec:implementation} records stack choices. Sections~\ref{sec:findings} and~\ref{sec:conclusion} discuss trade-offs and close.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Open government data and data portals.} Portals expose catalogues via APIs, yet usability and reuse remain hard for non-experts~\cite{nikiforova2021usability, molodtsov2024usability, wang2023ogd}. Most prior work does not treat \emph{catalogue-scale discovery} and \emph{grounded answering over downloaded files} as one problem; we do.

\textbf{RAG, decomposition, and hybrid search.} RAG~\cite{lewis2020rag, gao2023rag, huang2024rag} typically assumes a fixed document corpus. We instead use two levels: dataset records, then file content. Query decomposition and multi-step retrieval~\cite{xiong2021multihop, wolfson2020break, fu2021decomposing, zhu2024fanoutqa, ammann2025decomposition} motivate our planner; hybrid dense--sparse retrieval~\cite{lee2023hybrid, wang2024rag, zhuang2024promptreps, gao2023dense} motivates our catalogue index.

\textbf{Tools, tables, and orchestration.} Program- and tool-augmented LMs~\cite{gao2023pal, chen2022program, cheng2023binder, wang2024chainoftable, zhang2024reactable, zhang2025rlm} inform our analytic path. Multi-agent LLM surveys~\cite{li2024multiagent, yu2025agent} motivate a \emph{fixed} orchestration (plan $\rightarrow$ retrieve $\rightarrow$ select $\rightarrow$ execute $\rightarrow$ synthesize) suited to auditability in public-sector settings rather than open-ended agent loops.

\section{The AGORA Protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}

The \textbf{AGORA protocol} is the coordination layer that turns a national open-data catalogue and a natural-language question into a single attributed answer. Unlike decentralized protocols that coordinate agents on a graph through iterated sensitivity messages---e.g.\ the Ripple Effect Protocol (REP)~\cite{chopra2025ripple}---AGORA uses one orchestrator with \emph{shared stage contracts} and a \emph{fixed order} of operations so that retrieval over metadata, selective file access, and generation remain auditable (see also multi-agent LLM surveys~\cite{li2024multiagent, yu2025agent}). At its core, the protocol separates \textbf{what} each agent computes locally from \textbf{how} those outputs are threaded into a pipeline state that ends in synthesis.

This structure lets heterogeneous LLM-backed modules cooperate without ad hoc message passing: each stage consumes typed inputs and emits typed outputs; the orchestrator alone advances global state.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Agents reason.} Planner, selector, General, Technical, and synthesis modules each apply internal policies (LLM or hybrid LLM\,+\,tools) to their inputs---subqueries, hit lists, downloaded resources---and produce structured artifacts (plans, selections, evidence blocks).
  \item \textbf{Protocol coordinates.} The orchestrator enforces stage order, deduplicates catalogue hits across subqueries, routes each selected dataset to the correct evidence mode, concatenates evidence in a fixed order, and attaches citation fields so the final answer can point to portal records.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Workflow overview}
\label{sec:protocol-workflow}

Let $\mathcal{D}$ be the finite set of dataset identifiers exposed by the portal catalogue. Let $\mathcal{I}$ denote the \emph{indexed} catalogue: metadata records augmented with dense and lexical features for hybrid search (Section~\ref{sec:retrieval}). Let $Q$ be a user question.

A \textbf{run} is a mapping from $Q$ to an answer $A$ through five stages $s=1,\ldots,5$. The following is the abstract control flow; Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} instantiates it visually.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Plan.} A planner $\pi_{\mathrm{plan}}$ maps $Q$ to a plan $P=(\iota, \{q_\ell\}_{\ell=1}^{k})$, where $\iota$ is a short intent string and each $q_\ell$ is a retrieval-oriented subquery (possibly $k{=}1$).
  \item \textbf{Retrieve.} For each $\ell$, hybrid search over $\mathcal{I}$ returns a ranked hit list $H_\ell \subseteq \mathcal{D}$ (with scores and metadata). Hits may be deduplicated across $\ell$.
  \item \textbf{Select.} A selector $\pi_{\mathrm{sel}}$ maps $(Q, P, \{H_\ell\})$ to a set of triples $S=\{(d,m_d,r_d)\}$ with $d\in\mathcal{D}$, $m_d\in\{\mathrm{RAG},\mathrm{TECH}\}$, and optional rationale $r_d$. The selector may output $S=\emptyset$.
  \item \textbf{Execute evidence.} For each $(d,m_d)\in S$, an executor $\mathrm{Ex}_{m_d}$ downloads portal resources for $d$, extracts content, and returns an evidence block $e_d$ (passage-style for $\mathrm{RAG}$, structured or tool-assisted for $\mathrm{TECH}$).
  \item \textbf{Synthesize.} A synthesizer $\pi_{\mathrm{syn}}$ maps $(Q, P, \{e_d\}_{d\in S}, \mathrm{refs}(S))$ to a final text answer $A$ with citations, where $\mathrm{refs}(S)$ supplies titles, organizations, and URLs for datasets in $S$.
\end{enumerate}

\smallskip
\noindent\emph{Example.} For ``feasibility of a hotel in Lyon,'' $P$ might split into subqueries on tourism, infrastructure, and zoning; each $H_\ell$ overlaps partially with others; $S$ might keep three datasets with modes $(\mathrm{RAG},\mathrm{RAG},\mathrm{TECH})$; evidence blocks $e_d$ feed one synthesis step that cites each dataset URL.

\subsection{Coordination state and evidence aggregation}
\label{sec:protocol-coordination}

It is useful to name the \textbf{protocol state} carried forward. Let $\theta_1=P$ after planning; $\theta_2=(P,\{H_\ell\})$ after retrieval; $\theta_3=(P,S)$ after selection; $\theta_4=(P,\{e_d\}_{d\in S})$ after execution. Only $\theta_4$ and citation metadata enter synthesis.

\emph{Aggregation} is deliberately simple: evidence blocks are merged in a deterministic order (e.g., by subquery and then dataset id) into a single context for $\pi_{\mathrm{syn}}$:
\begin{equation}
  E \;=\; \bigoplus_{d\in S^{\downarrow}} e_d\,,
\end{equation}
where $S^{\downarrow}$ denotes $S$ with a fixed total order. The design goal is traceability: each $e_d$ remains attributable to $d$, so $A$ can reference concrete catalogue entries.

\emph{Routing} is the assignment $d \mapsto m_d$ induced by $\pi_{\mathrm{sel}}$. Equivalently, $S=\pi_{\mathrm{sel}}(Q,P,\{H_\ell\}_{\ell=1}^{k})$ aggregates all per-dataset decisions in one call. This is the step that realizes two-level retrieval: first choose \emph{which} datasets matter, then choose \emph{how} to read them.

\subsection{Implementation mapping}
\label{sec:protocol-implementation}

The protocol is implemented as a thin \textbf{orchestration layer} around pluggable backends: a vector store and BM25 index for $\mathcal{I}$, HTTP access to the portal API for resource URLs, and LLM clients for $\pi_{\mathrm{plan}}$, $\pi_{\mathrm{sel}}$, executors, and $\pi_{\mathrm{syn}}$. Transport (REST, SSE), caps on $|S|$ and resources per $d$, and hybrid search parameters are configuration knobs---analogous to swapping aggregation rules in other coordination frameworks---without changing the stage logic above.

\noindent\textit{Configuration interface} (illustrative):
\begin{small}
\begin{verbatim}
orchestrator = agora.configure(
    catalogue_index=weaviate_index,
    portal_client=ckan_api,
    planner=llm_planner,
    selector=llm_selector,
    general_agent=rag_executor,
    technical_agent=rlm_executor,
    synthesizer=llm_synthesis,
)
\end{verbatim}
\end{small}

\noindent\textit{Protocol execution} for one question:
\begin{small}
\begin{verbatim}
P = orchestrator.plan(Q)
H = [orchestrator.search(q) for q in P.subqueries]
S = orchestrator.select(Q, P, H)
E = [orchestrator.execute(d, mode) for (d, mode, _) in S]
A = orchestrator.synthesize(Q, P, E, refs=S)
\end{verbatim}
\end{small}

\noindent Streaming and usage accounting hook the same stages: each transition $\theta_s \!\to\! \theta_{s+1}$ may emit progress events and token counts (Section~\ref{sec:implementation}).

\subsection{Orchestrator and runtime modes}

The orchestrator is the only entry point: it invokes the planner, retrieval, selector, evidence agents, and synthesis in order. Two \textbf{user-visible modes} matter at a conceptual level: \emph{general} forces passage-style evidence for every selected dataset; \emph{technical} lets the selector assign each dataset to descriptive or analytic execution. Streaming updates (e.g., server-sent events) expose progress so users see \emph{which} datasets are being considered---a simple transparency device that matters for trust.

\subsection{Data flow}
\label{sec:dataflow}

Conceptually the pipeline maps \textbf{question} $\rightarrow$ \textbf{plan} (intent and retrieval-oriented subqueries) $\rightarrow$ \textbf{hits} per subquery from hybrid catalogue search $\rightarrow$ \textbf{selection} (which datasets matter, and which evidence mode) $\rightarrow$ \textbf{evidence blocks} per dataset $\rightarrow$ \textbf{one synthesized answer} with dataset titles and URLs carried forward for citation. Token and embedding tallies can be aggregated for cost awareness; Section~\ref{sec:implementation} lists the concrete stack.

\subsection{System interface and user experience}

The interface makes the two-path design legible: users choose a \textbf{mode}, watch \textbf{per-dataset progress} (dataset name, mode, short rationale, portal link), and receive a \textbf{single synthesized answer} with citations (Figures~\ref{fig:interface-modes}--\ref{fig:sample-output}). The intent is accessibility---non-experts should see \emph{where} answers come from without reading logs.

\section{Design Challenges}
\label{sec:challenges}

At scale, open-data QA raises recurring tensions: the catalogue is large but metadata is sparse; files are heterogeneous and heavy; and different questions need \emph{different} kinds of evidence. Table~\ref{tab:challenges} maps these tensions to design choices at a glance; we do not repeat every engineering knob here---those live in Section~\ref{sec:implementation}.

\begin{table}[t]
  \centering
  \caption{Main tensions at catalogue scale and the corresponding design responses.}
  \label{tab:challenges}
  \footnotesize
  \begin{tabular}{@{}p{0.46\columnwidth}p{0.46\columnwidth}@{}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Tension} & \textbf{Design response} \\
    \midrule
    Find relevant datasets in a noisy catalogue &
    Hybrid semantic--lexical search; planner emits subqueries for recall. \\
    Avoid indexing every raw file &
    Download resources only for selected datasets. \\
    Descriptive vs.\ analytic questions &
    Two evidence modes (passage-style vs.\ structured/tool-assisted). \\
    Long files, many formats &
    Chunking and similarity for text; parsers and extraction for tables/dates/docs. \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

In one sentence: \textbf{discovery} should be cheap and global; \textbf{reading} should be selective and mode-appropriate. That split keeps the framework honest about cost (storage, tokens, latency) while still supporting heterogeneous government data.

\section{Planner Agent}
\label{sec:planner}

The planner turns a user question into an \textbf{intent} and one or more \textbf{retrieval-oriented subqueries}. Broad questions split into themes (e.g., feasibility of a hotel in Lyon $\rightarrow$ tourism, infrastructure); narrow questions stay a single subquery. Figure~\ref{fig:subqueries} shows the idea: each subquery drives an independent catalogue search (Section~\ref{sec:retrieval}). Implementation uses a structured LLM call with JSON output and a safe fallback plan if parsing fails (details in Section~\ref{sec:implementation}).

\section{Retrieval}
\label{sec:retrieval}

\textbf{Hybrid search} over embedded catalogue text combines dense similarity with lexical matching---a standard neural-IR recipe~\cite{lee2023hybrid, wang2024rag} applied here to dataset titles, descriptions, tags, and organization names. Each subquery yields a ranked list of datasets; duplicates are merged across subqueries before selection. No generative model runs at this stage: retrieval is purely search, which keeps costs predictable and explanations simpler.

%------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Dataset Selector Agent}
\label{sec:selector}

The selector is the \textbf{gate} between noisy retrieval and expensive file access. It (i)~\emph{drops} irrelevant hits---including returning nothing when the portal simply has no suitable data---and (ii)~\emph{assigns} each kept dataset to \textbf{descriptive} evidence (passage-style reading) or \textbf{analytic} evidence (structured inspection and light computation). The assignment is explained in short natural language for the UI. This design reduces spurious downloads and makes the system's behavior inspectable.

%------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{General (RAG) Agent}
\label{sec:general}

The \textbf{General} path answers from \emph{text}: it downloads selected resources, extracts content, optionally applies chunking and similarity-based retrieval over chunks~\cite{yepes2024chunking, qu2024semantic, chen2024dense}, and produces a self-contained evidence block for synthesis. The guiding principle is that a \emph{sample} of the right passages can suffice for overview-style questions.

%------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Technical Agent}
\label{sec:technical}

The \textbf{Technical} path targets questions that need \emph{lookup or aggregation} over structured content: tables, records, and sometimes text extracted from less structured files. The agent builds a compact view of schema and previews, then uses a \textbf{code-capable} model with a \textbf{sandbox} to iterate until it produces structured evidence (Figure~\ref{fig:rlm-code}): this is the same conceptual pattern as recursive language models~\cite{zhang2025rlm}, but applied in the open-data setting. A pure-prompt fallback remains available when execution is disabled.

\section{Synthesis Agent}
\label{sec:synthesis}

\textbf{Synthesis} merges the evidence blocks into one \textbf{user-facing answer} in the question's language, with citations and dataset links. Prompting is tuned so the model speaks as \emph{portal} data, not ``internal pipeline'' data---a small but important detail for trustworthy UX. The synthesis step is a single generative call over concatenated evidence; Section~\ref{sec:implementation} notes the concrete LM stack.

%------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Implementation Notes}
\label{sec:implementation}

This section collects \textbf{concrete} choices so the paper stays high-level elsewhere. The stack is Python/FastAPI; \textbf{Weaviate} holds the hybrid index with \textbf{self-provided} embeddings; \textbf{DSPy}~\cite{khattab2023dspy} wires planner, selector, General (\texttt{Predict}), Technical (\texttt{RLM} with REPL or \texttt{Predict} fallback), and synthesis (\texttt{ChainOfThought}). Ingestion walks the data.gouv.fr API, builds a rich text field per dataset, and batch-embeds. Extraction uses common parsers plus the \textbf{Unstructured} toolkit for many document and spreadsheet types, with size guards and fallbacks for oversized files.

The orchestrator tracks \textbf{LLM and embedding usage} per stage and can estimate cost when model names are known. Defaults (caps on datasets per run, resources per dataset, chunk sizes, hybrid $\alpha$) are tuned for stability on large catalogues; the open source tree is the authoritative place for exact values.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:findings}

Three design tensions recur in practice. \textbf{First,} decomposition and multi-query retrieval diversify recall across sparse catalogue text, but each extra subquery adds latency; the planner should stay retrieval-oriented, not over-split. \textbf{Second,} the selector's split between passage-style and analytic evidence mirrors a real distinction between ``what does this dataset say?'' and ``what is the value or count in the table?''---getting that split wrong either wastes tokens or misses answers. \textbf{Third,} transparency is partly a UX problem: streaming progress and citation-first prompts help users trust outputs; a rigorous empirical evaluation (retrieval metrics, human judgments, ablations) remains important future work for this line of research.

Looking ahead, the linear pipeline invites \textbf{quality-control stages} before synthesis (e.g., cleaning noisy descriptions at index time, validating evidence blocks) and a richer \textbf{capability registry} so new tools or domains can plug in without rewriting the orchestrator. Both directions fit the framework framing: keep the orchestration stable, swap or improve modules.

%------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Open government data will remain underused while catalogues and files are disconnected from the questions people actually ask. AGORA proposes a \textbf{single coherent story}: search the catalogue like an IR system, read only what matters, ground answers in sources, and separate descriptive from analytic evidence when the data demand it. Our reference build on data.gouv.fr shows that this story can be implemented end-to-end; the open framework is meant to support other portals, evaluations, and extensions without losing the accessibility that motivated the design. The next step we care most about is \textbf{rigorous evaluation} alongside the architecture---so that improvements to open-data access can be measured, not only described.
